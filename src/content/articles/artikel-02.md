I once had a lover who told me I should take a walk in the nearby graveyard. I didn't go there until we split up. I had almost forgotten it even existed. Even though, along with Burger King, it is the only "attraction" in the area where I live. To be honest, I can't remember my first time at the Waldfriedhof, but it must have been special since I started visiting more frequently after that.

The Munich Waldfriedhof is huge — the second largest of its kind in Germany. I liked the idea of not being able to see every corner of it, thanks to my poor sense of direction and unstructured way of approaching things. As it turned out, I mostly took the same paths without realizing it immediately. It felt as if certain objects repeatedly caught my attention, absorbing me and leading my way — almost like someone calling my name. These objects varied from certain sculptures or sun-bleached portraits to rotten flowers or just a special kind of vegetation. I started to collect them with my camera. But after a while, I had to admit these photos had more in common with a self-portrait than a portrait of this special place.

Later, I realized that my fascination with graveyards and funerals might be an obsession with pathos, mysticism, and darkness, but something else lies beneath. It's about "bearing witness." I need the graveyard to believe that life actually ends. I need the funeral to truly understand that a loved one now belongs to the past. And in all this finality, instead of being tired of life, a strange kind of affirmation takes its place.

So I decided to capture the silent witnesses of the graveyard. Through their varying designs and materials, they mark the passage of time while remaining perfectly still. Benches are in between the people who died and the people who miss them — in between life and death.
